Brain Structure

Lower level brain structures:

  • Brain stem: The brain stem is the oldest part of the brain, beginning where the spinal cord swells and enters the skull. It is responsible for automatic survival functions.

  • Medulla: The base of the brain stem, controls heartbeat and breathing.

  • Reticular Formation: A nerve network in the brainstorm that plays an important role in wakefulness and arousal.

  • Thalamus: The brain's sensory switchboard, located on top of the brain stem. It directs messages to the sensory receiving areas in the cortex and transmits replies to the cerebellum and medulla.

  • Cerebellum: The "little brain" attached to the rear of the brain stem. It helps coordinate voluntary movements and balance.


The limbic system:

  • Hypothalamus: A neural structure lying below the thalamus. It directs several maintenance activities (eating, drinking, body temperature), helps govern the endocrine system via the pituitary gland, and is linked to emotion.

  • Hippocampus: A neural center located in the limbic system that helps process new memories for long term storage.

  • Amygdala: The two lima-bean sized neural clusters in the limbic system that control emotional responses, such as fear and anger.


The cerebral cortex

  • The cerebral cortex: The intricate fabric of interconnected neural cells covering the cerebral hemispheres. The body's ultimate control and information processing center.

  • Corpus callosum: The large band of neural fibers connecting the two brain hemispheres and carrying messages between them. Damaging this can cause the two hemispheres to not communicate properly.

  • Frontal lobes: The portion of the cerebral cortex lying just behind the forehead that is involved in judgement and planning. Damage to this area can cause a person to lose their ability to plan for the future.

  • Parietal lobes: The portion of the cerebral cortex lying at the top of the head and toward the rear; includes somatosensory cortex and general areas for processing information. Damage to this area can cause a person to lose their ability to process some sensory information.

  • Occipital lobes: The portion of the cerebral cortex lying at the back of the head; includes the primary visual processing areas of the brain. Damage to this area can cause a person to lose their ability to process visual information.

  • Temporal lobes: The portion of the cerebral cortex lying roughly above the ears; includes the auditory areas of the brain. Damage to this area can cause a person to lose their ability to process auditory information.

  • Longitudinal fissure: The long crevice that separates the brain into the left and right hemispheres.

  • Motor cortex: Strip of cortex at the rear of the frontal lobes that controls voluntary movements. Damage to this area can cause a person to lose their ability to control their movements.

  • Somatosensory cortex: strip of brain tissue at the front of the parietal lobes that registers and processes body touch and movement sensations. Damage to this area can cause a person to lose their ability to process some sensory information.
